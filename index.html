What is ocean acidification

“Ocean acidification” is a decrease in the pH of ocean water, driven by CO2 reacting with the water to form a weak acid. The ocean is currently a weak “alkaline” – the opposite of an acid – so the phrase “acidification” does not mean that the ocean is becoming an acid. Instead, it means that the ocean is becoming less alkaline and moving closer to a “neutral” pH.

Carbon is constantly cycling between the atmosphere, land and ocean. However, carbon is currently entering the ocean faster than it is leaving, largely due to human activity. There are two main ways that the ocean takes up carbon from the atmosphere – the “biological” carbon pump, and the “physical” carbon pump.

Sabine discusses how ocean acidification was first “discovered”:

The physical carbon pump – also known as the “solubility” pump – describes the process of CO2 from the atmosphere entering the ocean through diffusion. Diffusion is the movement of molecules across a “concentration gradient” from areas of low pressure to areas of high pressure. This is a basic law of physics Sabine explains:

“The physical pump is a simple law of thermodynamics, or a physical property of any gases that a gas will always move from a high concentration to low concentration. So you've got CO2 in the atmosphere, and you've got CO2 in the ocean. And whichever is higher – it will go from that medium to the other medium.”

Human activity is causing atmospheric CO2 levels to rise, making the level of CO2 – more specifically the “partial pressure” – higher in the atmosphere than in the ocean. This means that, via diffusion, CO2 is moving from the atmosphere into the ocean.

It takes roughly one year for a change in atmospheric CO2 to balance out with ocean levels through diffusion alone. However, there are many factors that can affect diffusion.

For example, if the water is choppy and there are lots of breaking waves, the bubbles of air mixed into the water will speed up diffusion of CO2 into the water. During high-intensity winds, these bubbles can be responsible for half of the CO2 transfer between the air and ocean.

This is because breaking waves increase the surface area between the water and air, Sabine explains:

“Water is a very viscous fluid, and the exchange [of gasses] between the air and the water surface is very difficult because of surface tension and other properties of water. A breaking wave that injects air into the water creates a bubble – and the smaller bubbles in particular have a lot of surface area. So there's a lot of opportunity to exchange, rather than just like a calm lake surface that has very limited surface area.”

Temperature can also affect the solubility pump. It is well established that cold water can hold more CO2 than warm water. (This is for the same reason that fizzy drinks tend to go flat more quickly in warmer weather.) This means that ocean acidification can be “more drastic” in colder regions, Dr Ramessur tells Carbon Brief.

It also means that as the planet warms, the oceans are able to hold less CO2. One study estimates that by the end of the 21st century, warming will reduce the uptake of anthropogenic CO2 by the ocean by 9-15%.

Temperature can also influence ocean circulation. Ocean water is constantly circulating through different depths of the ocean. However as temperatures rise, the layers in the ocean are separating out more – a process called stratification. This can alter the concentration gradient of CO2 between the ocean and atmosphere and change the rate of diffusion.

The second way that CO2 can enter the water is through the “biological carbon pump”. This process is driven by microscopic plants – collectively called “phytoplankton” – that live near the surface of the water and take in CO2 for photosynthesis.

Phytoplankton are often eaten by zooplankton or larger organisms, which in turn are eaten by even larger organisms further up the food chain. The plant and animal life eventually dies and sinks to the bottom of the ocean, burying the carbon with them. During this process, around 10% of the total carbon biomass ends up as fecal matter or dead cells that also sink to the bottom of the ocean

Only around 0.2 Pg of carbon makes it from the surface of the ocean to the depths each year. However, Sabine explains that the carbon could be stored in sediments for millenia or longer, making it an important biological carbon sink:

“On the land when a plant dies, it just falls to the ground, decomposes and releases its co2. However in the ocean when that plant dies, it sinks down into the ocean where it decomposes. It takes that carbon and it releases it down in the deep ocean. The circulation of the ocean is very slow, so that carbon may be stuck in the ocean down the deep ocean or 1000 years or more... And what that does is that creates a gradient so you have low co2 in the surface, high co2 and the deep.”

The chemistry of ocean acidification

Ocean acidification is driven by carbon dioxide reacting with water to form carbonic acid. Changes in acidity are usually calculated using pH – a measure of the concentration of hydrogen ions (H+) in a liquid. The pH scale runs from zero to 14, where a pH of seven is “neutral”, below seven is acid and above seven is alkaline. This means that an increase in H+ ions makes a liquid more acidic and gives it a lower pH.

pH is “a fundamental chemical property that controls a whole bunch of different processes in the ocean”, Sabine tells Carbon Brief, adding that “a lot of the chemical reactions that occur in the ocean are a function of pH”.

The ocean has a pH above seven, making it an alkaline. However, its pH has declined from 8.2 to 8.1 on average since the industrial era, due to human activity. This seemingly small change actually corresponds to a 30% increase in the concentration of H+ ions because the pH scale is “logarithmic”.

Ocean acidification begins with the reaction between water and carbon dioxide to form a weak acid called carbonic acid.

The carbonic acid breaks down into a bicarbonate ion and a hydrogen ion in a process called dissociation.

The bicarbonate ion can then dissociate again into a carbonate ion and another hydrogen ion.

The bicarbonate and hydrogen are ions because they have a positive or negative charge, shown by the + and - symbols. The chemical equation for this reaction is shown in the diagram.

Sabine explains that these ions combined are called “dissolved inorganic carbon”:

“CO2 enters the ocean, as a gas, but then it reacts with the water molecules to form carbonic acid, bicarbonate and carbonate ions. All these different ions together form what's called the total CO2 or the dissolved inorganic carbon of the oceans.”

He adds that when CO2 molecules enter the ocean, around 99% of them react with water molecules to form these other “species”. This keeps the concentration gradient – the difference between atmospheric and oceanic levels of CO2 – high, driving further diffusion of CO2 into the water. This is “why the oceans are so efficient at absorbing CO2 from the atmosphere”, according to Sabine.

The chemical reactions are reversible, as shown by the double-headed arrows, meaning they can progress either forwards or backwards. The aim is to reach a “balanced equilibrium”, where none of the products or reactants are present in excessively high levels.

In the modern-day climate, for example, CO2 is constantly entering the ocean. To prevent levels of CO2 from building up, the reaction between CO2 and water speeds up, and the reaction is pushed “forwards”. This drives the production of more H+ ions, causing the ocean to acidify.

However, the reaction can also be pushed “backwards” if a body of water has naturally high levels of bicarbonate ions or carbonate ions. The concentration of these ions is referred to as the “alkalinity” of the ocean and is generally higher in regions with natural deposits of calcium carbonate in the surrounding land.

For example, limestone is a sedimentary rock composed mainly of calcium carbonate (CaCO3), usually in the form of calcite or aragonite. Weathering of limestone can release calcium carbonate into the water, where it can break down to form calcium and bicarbonate ions.

The resistance of the water to a change in pH is called “buffer capacity”. Sabine explains the idea of buffer capacity more clearly below:

“If you were to just add an acid to [ocean] water, the pH change in the ocean would be smaller than it would be in say freshwater, because there's a lot of bicarbonate already in the water that's buffering it.”
“Sabine talking head - buffering capacity. 23:10”

However, as CO2 continues to enter the water, the buffer capacity of the oceans is dropping. Under a high emission scenario, it is expected that buffer capacity could decrease by 34% between 2000-2100, reducing the ability of the ocean to resist a change in pH.

Where is ocean acidification happening?

The rate of ocean acidification is not constant around the planet and factors including temperature, ocean buffer capacity and land-based pollution can affect local acidification levels.

CO2 dissolves more easily in cold water than warm water, making polar regions more vulnerable to ocean acidification than tropical regions. The Arctic is the most susceptible region to ocean acidification in the world, partly due to the cold temperatures.

The Arctic is also vulnerable to acidification because it has a naturally low buffer capacity. Buffer capacity, as discussed earlier, is the ability of a liquid to resist changes in pH. For oceans, the ability to resist acidification is largely determined by the levels of bicarbonate ions, carbonate ions and calcium carbonate in the water.

In the Arctic, levels of calcite and aragonite – both types of calcium carbonate – are naturally low. Furthermore, fresh water from melting ice sheets dilutes the concentrations of calcite and aragonite. This means that the Arctic is unable to easily resist drops in pH.

Ocean acidification can also be exacerbated by an influx of nutrients, human pollution and freshwater from the land, which makes coastlines particularly vulnerable, Gattuso tells Carbon Brief:

““Pollution, for example untreated sewage, contributes a lot of organic matter – particulate and dissolved. Then you get lots of microbes respiring for this organic matter of generating CO2. The CO2 generated by land pollution can contribute significantly at a local scale to ocean acidification.””

Similarly, nitrogen and phosphorus from fertilisers running into the ocean can cause “eutrophication” – when an influx of nutrients into the water causes an initial bloom of phytoplankton, Sabine explains:

The phytoplankton can blanket the surface of the water, suffocating the marine life beneath and causing low oxygen levels – known as “hypoxia”. In some instances this can kill marine life, which then decomposes and releases carbon into the water, causing acidification.

When the phytoplankton die near coasts, they are often unable to sink deep down into the ocean as they would do in the open water, and instead decompose near the surface of the water, releasing more carbon and causing the surface water to acidify further, Sabine explains:

Sabine adds that rivers can affect coastal pH levels – either increasing or decreasing the pH depending on the material that they are carrying:

“Organic carbon that gets washed off of the land into the rivers. You think about these brown rivers – such as the Mississippi River – that are full of organic matter. As that decomposes it releases CO2… It really depends on the type of soil and rocks that the river is flowing through that determines whether a river contributes to acidification, or actually some rivers can reduce acidification. If you have rivers that flow through carbonate material and it's dissolving those carbonates, you can have these high alkalinity rivers. If you have rivers that flow through carbonate material and it's dissolving those carbonates, you can have these high alkalinity rivers. ”

Ramessur adds that fresh water – which has a “neutral” pH around seven – will decrease ocean pH. He adds that climate change is expected to bring “more rain, more runoff and more discharge” which will further contribute to this effect.

Ocean acidification can be exacerbated by the “upwelling” of CO2-rich water rising from the deep ocean, which causes pH to drop. Gattuso notes that such upwellings are particularly prevalent around coastlines:

“An upwelling is an area where deep water, due to ocean circulation, is pushed to the surface. CO2 is more abundant in deep waters usually, because of microbial respiration. So when deep water is brought to the surface, it acidifies the water because there is low pH. It also brings nutrients – nitrates and phosphates – triggering phytoplankton blooms. There are very active areas that are along the west coast of the US, South America, the west coast of Africa. Those upwellings are places where there is a very active fishing industry.”

Gattuso adds that near the coast, emissions of sulphur dioxide (SO2) from ships and factories can produce acid rain, which can also exacerbate acidification. He says that this effect is “not significant”, but that “it can be measured close to very industrialised areas”.

Carbon dioxide can also enter the ocean through volcanic vents on the sea bed that can affect entire columns of water. However, the effect that these have on overall acidification levels is negligible.

Impacts on corals and calcifying organisms

Other impacts on marine life and societies

Ocean acidification can affect marine life in ways that are less immediately noticeable, but no less damaging, than the impact on coral reefs and calcifying organisms.

For example, acidification can reduce the ability of marine plankton to form their skeletons through a similar mechanism as the weakening of corals and shelled organisms. This can drive a range of evolutionary changes – for example by altering the competitive fitness between different phytoplankton species and thereby changing phytoplankton community structures.

Acidification can also reduce the rate of photosynthesis in plants and plankton – an impact that is often exacerbated by warming. Hassoun explains the damage caused by changes in phytoplankton populations:

“Phytoplankton are actually the base of the marine trophic chain. And when these little creatures are discurbed, then the whole marine trophic chain reaction – up to marine mammals – can also be influenced.”

For example, phytoplankton are also the ocean’s main source of certain types of fatty acids, which are important for the growth and reproduction in creatures like copepods. Scientists have found that low pH can change the ratio of different types of fatty acids in phytoplankton when they are exposed to lower pH water.

In addition to the wider impacts from the food chain, ocean acidification can also have a wide range of direct impacts on larger species. Acidification has been shown to impact immune systems in some marine organisms, making them more vulnerable to harmful pathogens.

An increase in H+ ion concentration can also disrupt the balance of ions in the membranes of a fish’s gills, and can cause physical changes to the gills. Acidification can also affect the metabolism of fish – forcing them to use more energy to maintain their own acid-base balance.

Many species also find it more difficult to find shelter as a result of decreasing pH. This partly because the acidification can affect their habitats – for example, by damaging corals or pH-sensitive plants. It is also because acidification leads to “info-disruption”, in which a fish’s senses – such as sight and smell – are altered by the changing pH.

Poertnet explains that this is due to changes in organisms’ neurotransmitters – the “chemical messengers” that carry messages from neurons:

“There is an impact on neurotransmitter metabolism and function which can reduce the capacity of individuals to have proper orientation when moving, to perceive and find prey or to respond properly to the presence of predators.”

Neurotransmitter damage can drive behaviour changes – such as shifting levels of aggression that can reshape hierarchies within species, or changes in the ability of species to avoid predators – that can affect population numbers.

Acidification can also impact reproduction and early development in fish. Studies have shown that fish spawning decreases in more acidic waters, and that fish eggs and larvae are negatively affected by ocean acidification. There is also research suggesting that juvenile fish are more sensitive to changes in pH than adult fish.

Ramessur tells Carbon Brief that these impacts on marine life could have a knock-on impact on people through both tourism and disruption to the fishing industry, resulting in an “economic disaster” for many communities:

““Ocean acidification can have an impact on both fisheries and tourism… If you rely on coral reefs for the tourism industry, and ocean acidification causes destruction of the reefs, you will obviously have a problem.””

For example, pteropods are a key food source for North Pacific salmon, which is responsible for 90% of the salmon consumed by humans. However, Gatusso tells Carbon Brief that there are worries over the stability of pteropod populations that could threaten the quality of the salmon:

“In the North Pacific, pteropods at some times of the year represent nearly 90% of the food for North Pacific salmon. There are worries about the potential disappearance of pteropods in the north, especially because of its impact on salmon. And we are not well equipped to make predictions on what could occur because obviously an ecological niche, never stays empty. So if the pteropods disappear, they will be replaced by another zooplankton type, but what is unknown is whether this new food for salmon will have the same benefits, food quality etc.”

There are also direct human impacts of ocean acidification on people. For example, some species of toxic algae have been found to benefit from ocean acidification – especially near coastlines.

‘The deadly trio’ – Acidification, warming and deoxygenation

Ocean acidification alone can have a significant impact on marine life, as shown above. However, this is not the only stressor facing marine life today. More than 90% of the excess heat released due greenhouse gases is stored in the oceans, which have warmed by around 1C due to human activity.

Many species have a limited thermal tolerance in which they can survive. If climate change increases temperatures beyond a species’ tolerance, they can experience negative impacts from heat stress, and so they are forced to migrate away from their usual habitat to cooler waters.

Marine migration is also causing problems with invasive species. Research shows that endemic species – those that live in only one region – are generally more sensitive to the negative impacts of climate change, and so they are at risk of being outcompeted by invading species.

The majority of fish species respond negatively to warming temperatures, research shows, and so many fisheries are already seeing these impacts. Research also shows that ocean heatwaves have become 34% more likely over the past century.

For species that are unable to leave to cooler waters, warming can be very detrimental. For example, ocean warming means that severe coral reef bleaching events now five times more frequent than 40 years ago, and have driven a decrease in global coral reefs.

Warmer water is also able to hold less oxygen, and oceans have seen a 2% decrease in oxygen content of the past 50 years. Low oxygen levels can affect species metabolism, and has already led to the formation of “dead zones” in which many marine species cannot survive.

These three impacts – warming, deoxygenation and acidification – are often found together. For example eutrophication – an influx of nutrients into the water, as discussed earlier – often combine the impacts of deoxygenation, acidification and warming to create “dead zones”.

Poertner explains that this combination of impacts due to eutrophication is particularly significant in coastal areas:

“Often, the development of hypoxia and high CO2 levels go together in the warming seas, trends which may be exacerbated further by anthropogenic eutrophication, especially in coastal areas”.

Research also shows that their combined impacts are additive. This means that the combination of these three stressors can negatively impact marine life more than a single stressor can.

Poertner has conducted extensive research into the concurrent impacts of these three stressors, and tells Carbon Brief that ocean acidification is “exacerbating hypoxia”, and “can narrow the thermal range exploited by a species”.

A special report from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change notes that the combined effects of warming, deoxygenation, and acidification could impact the body size, growth, reproduction and mortality of fish. These factors can all increase the risk of population decline, the report says.

Hassoun tells Carbon Brief that even phytoplankton are impacted by a range of environmental factors:

““Phytoplankton are very sensitive and fragile creatures. The majority of them are floating on the surface of seawater, and they are affected by not only seawater biogeochemistry and phenomena, but also by atmospheric chemistry as well. So the increase of CO2 in the atmosphere, the increase of temperature etc.”

Dr Helena Martins, science communicator in the Rossby Centre at the Swedish Meteorological and Hydrological Institute told Carbon Brief in a guest post on potential “ocean tipping points” that the combination of these three impacts could cause “ecosystem-wide shifts”:

““While these different processes are individually a danger to marine life, in combination with other threats – such as overfishing, high nutrient input from land and invasive species – they have the potential to cause ecosystem-wide regime shifts.”

A recent paper referring to acidification, warming and deoxygenation as “the deadly trio” finds that under an extremely high emission scenario, extreme acidification events, marine heatwaves and hypoxic events will become “potentially more devastating”.

This trend is concerning, Gatusso notes, because the five mass extinctions of ocean life that occurred in the past all involved a combination of these three stressors:

“The five mass extinctions which occurred in the past all had similar ingredients in the ocean – warming, acidification, and loss of oxygen. Extreme conditions in these variables - with other parameters of course - led to mass extinctions. I’m not reducing mass extinctions to those three things, but clearly a correlation is here. And this is exactly the recipe that will occur in the future.”

As such, it is important to monitor the impact of all three factors together, rather than studying them in isolation, Hassoun explains:

“It's important when we talk about ocean acidification to monitor this phenomenon, not as something that is happening alone, but as something that is happening in parallel with other stressors… There are many experimental studies about the potential effects of ocean acidification on phytoplankton, zooplankton, shrimp, many types of fish, etc. These experiments are interesting, and they can tell us so much. However, the majority of them are tackling the problem of ocean acidification solely, which is not enough. We need to have this multi stressor experimental studies.”
